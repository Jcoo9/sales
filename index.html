Structural "We have to buy media through our internal trade desk." ›
Why reps lose here
They treat this as a hard no and back off. It's not a no — it's a routing issue. The trade desk is a buyer, not a blocker. Most reps don't know how to sell through it.
What to say
Response"Totally understand — that's actually pretty common at your scale. Help me understand how your trade desk evaluates new inventory sources. Is it purely on price and scale, or do they consider things like audience exclusivity and brand safety certifications? Because that changes how I'd position this for them."
Next moves
Ask for an intro to the trade desk contact — you're not going around them, you're helping them route it correctly
Find out if there's a private marketplace or PMP path — trade desks love PMPs because it still looks like programmatic to them
Offer to put together a one-pager the planner can share internally — do the work for them
Price "You're too expensive." ›
Why reps lose here
They immediately defend the price or start discounting. Both moves signal that the price wasn't justified in the first place. "Too expensive" almost always means "I don't see the value yet" — not "the number is too high."
What to say
Response"Compared to what? I want to make sure we're comparing the right things. If you're looking at a lower CPM somewhere else, I'd want to understand what their audience looks like against yours, what their viewability and completion rates are, and what kind of reporting and optimization support comes with it. Because on a cost-per-outcome basis, we usually look very different."
Next moves
Never discount first — ask what budget they do have and build a package around it
Reframe from CPM to cost-per-outcome — viewability, completed views, site visits, whatever their KPI is
Ask what a successful campaign looks like for them — anchor the conversation on results, not rate
Approval "The client didn't approve you." ›
Why reps lose here
Reps accept this and disappear. But "the client didn't approve" usually means the agency didn't advocate for you — because they didn't have enough to make the case. The client rarely has strong opinions about specific publishers unless someone put one in front of them.
What to say
Response"That's helpful to know. Can I ask — was it that the client specifically said no to us, or that we weren't on the approved list they were working from? Because those are pretty different situations. If it's the latter, I'd love to help you build the case to get us added. I can put together something specifically for [client name] that speaks to their audience and their goals."
Next moves
Offer to build a custom one-pager the agency can send to the client — make it easy for them to advocate for you
Ask if there's a path to get on the approved vendor list for future campaigns
Find out who at the brand actually controls vendor approvals — sometimes it's the media manager, sometimes it's legal or procurement
